Accounting

Ph.D. in Accounting

Visit the admissions calendar for admissions information and application deadlines.

Why a Ph.D. in accounting?

A Ph.D. in accounting is for aspiring accounting academics. An academic career is a rewarding one for accountants. An accounting professor will ask and answer interesting research questions, mentor future business professionals, and serve the community where they live and work. Further, accounting professors are often very well compensated. Recent Ph.D. graduates from our program have starting compensation packages that range from approximately $175,000 to $275,000.

Why the University of South Carolina?

  • Top university for experimental research across all topic areas (BYU Rankings)
  • Extensive mentoring and collaboration with faculty who are publishing in top-tier journals
  • Tuition waiver, generous stipend and financial support for conferences

The School of Accounting in the Moore School has established a national reputation for academic research using experimental methods.Although historically known for excellence in behavioral research, our faculty conducts research in all areas of accounting using many different research approaches. The result is publications in the top academic accounting journals and also in many top academic journals in related fields such as finance, economics, psychology, organizational behavior and information systems. Students in the PhD program receive world-class training in conducting and analyzing research that relies on experiments. The BYU rankings put the School of Accounting as the number 4 overall experimental research school. Of particular note, the  Moore School houses a behavioral lab wherein students and faculty can conduct experiments in a controlled lab setting. Doctoral students are able to investigate financial accounting, managerial accounting, taxation, auditing and information systems topics using this new and promising research approach.

Incoming students should anticipate the Ph.D. program taking five years to complete. Funding includes a generous stipend along with health insurance. Funding is also available to support students attending conferences and doing research for their dissertations. For non-native English speakers, preference is given to individuals with school or work experience in an English-speaking country.

Student and Faculty Collaboration

Ph.D students have the opportunity to interact and collaborate extensively with faculty members in the accounting school. Below are examples of published academic articles where current faculty and Ph.D. program graduates worked together as co-authors. Many of these projects began while the graduates were still completing their Ph.D. studies. The relationships developed during these collaborations extend beyond graduation and enable more productive research throughout the students' entire careers.

  • Superior-Subordinate Divergence in Controllability Judgments by Jonathan Gay (alumni), Scott B. Jackson (faculty), Nate Waddoups (alumni), and Grazia Xiong (alumni)
  • The effect of reward frequency on performance under cash rewards and tangible rewards by Drew Newman (faculty), Ivo Tafkov, Nate Waddoups (alumni), and Grazia Xiong (alumni)
  • The Effect of Personalized Communication on Investor Earnings Fixation by Paul Black (alumni), Kevin Jackson, Stephen Rowe, and Aaron Zimbelman (faculty)
  • Audit firm tenure disclosure and nonprofessional investors' perceptions of auditor independence: The mitigating effect of partner rotation disclosure by Sarah Judge (alumni), Brian Goodson, and Chad Stefaniak (faculty)

Accounting Program of Study

The Ph.D. concentration in accounting provides students with the skills necessary to become productive scholars and teachers at major universities. Initially, all doctoral students complete a core curriculum that provides skills in research design and statistical analysis. Students also complete doctoral seminars in financial accounting, managerial accounting, auditing, and other areas as interests align. These seminars are led by faculty who are experts in their respective fields.

Students must also complete four doctoral seminars:

  • ACCT 832 Doctoral Seminar in Accounting Research - Seminar for beginning doctoral students that provides an overview of research topic areas, methods and designs currently used in accounting
  • ACCT 833 Doctoral Seminar in Financial Accounting - Research methods and issues related to financial accounting topics
  • ACCT 834 Doctoral Seminar in Managerial Accounting Research methods and issues related to managerial accounting topics
  • ACCT 835 Doctoral Seminar in Auditing and Accounting Information Systems Research methods and issues related to auditing and accounting information systems topic

In addition, all doctoral students complete a supervised research project at the end of their first and year of study.

Typically, doctoral students take cognate courses in psychology, management information systems, finance or organizational behavior, although it is possible to study in other areas. Unlike many heavily structured doctoral programs, each doctoral student has considerable latitude in determining a program of study that fits the individual's unique academic interests.
